1. The Two Measurement Frames

Every data collection run produces two types of signal simultaneously. It's important to understand the difference, because the validity claims are different for each.

Frame A — AI Brand Monitor

Here, the AI system is the subject. We are measuring what a specific LLM (or set of LLMs) says about your brand, cites about your brand, and associates with your brand. The claim is simple and unambiguous: "ChatGPT mentions your brand in 38% of responses to this category of queries." That is a direct observation. No proxy, no inference about human attitudes.

This frame is useful because AI systems are increasingly a primary distribution channel for brand information. What AI says about you shapes what people hear — regardless of whether AI "thinks" anything about you in a meaningful sense.

Frame B — AI Market Research

Here, the AI system is the instrument. We use it to simulate a research study: the Cartesian product of audience personas × research intents × AI platforms produces a structured dataset of how different synthetic respondents characterize, consider, and evaluate your brand.

The validity argument for Frame B does not require AI responses to be perfect proxies for human attitudes. It requires AI responses to be a credible, structured, and auditable source of market signal for a specific and growing set of decisions. The argument: for high-consideration decisions where AI is already in the research path, measuring what AI tells prospective buyers is measuring the research channel itself — not a proxy for it.

Frame B is most valid for: financial services, insurance, B2B software, healthcare, professional services, and other categories where AI-assisted research is documented. It is least valid for impulse categories and commodity goods where AI is not in the decision journey. We document this distinction explicitly in every study.

2. Data Collection Architecture

The Persona × Intent × Platform matrix

Each study begins by defining three dimensions:

  • Personas: demographic profiles (age, region, expertise level, decision context) that prime the AI to respond as a specific type of research respondent
  • Intents: the research questions asked — awareness queries, comparison queries, decision queries, validation queries
  • Platforms: the AI systems queried — typically ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude, Gemini, and Google AI

A standard study runs 30 replications per cell to establish statistical stability. Typical run sizes: 500–2,000 inference calls. All calls are cost-gated before execution — the client approves the estimated cost before any inference runs.

One question per prompt

Each inference call is designed to answer exactly one research question. Multi-question prompts that would allow a single LLM response to feed multiple KPIs simultaneously are avoided — they create correlated measurement error and make the results non-independent. If KPI #6 and KPI #7 draw from the same response, they share sampling variance and cannot be treated as independent measures.

Organic vs. elicited prompts

We strictly distinguish two prompt types and report them separately:

  • Organic prompts: brand is not in the prompt. Example: "Which Swiss insurance is best for young professionals?" — measures whether AI spontaneously mentions your brand
  • Elicited prompts: brand is in the prompt. Example: "Describe [Brand] in 2–3 sentences." — measures how AI characterizes your brand when asked

KPIs derived from organic prompts measure presence. KPIs derived from elicited prompts measure characterization. They are not interchangeable.

4. Validity Boundaries — What We Claim and What We Don't

We believe in stating the limits explicitly. A methodology document that doesn't acknowledge its own boundaries isn't a methodology document — it's marketing.

  • We claim: AI responses are a reliable, auditable, and structurally important signal for brand perception in AI-mediated research journeys
  • We claim: The Cartesian product of personas × intents × platforms produces a structured and reproducible dataset that can support brand strategy decisions
  • We do not claim: AI responses are equivalent to, or a perfect substitute for, primary human research
  • We do not claim: Publisher Lift (KPI #16) is causal. It measures correlation between media placements and AI citation changes. Causality would require experimental design with control groups we do not currently implement.
  • We do not claim: Results generalize across AI platforms. ChatGPT at 38% SoA and Claude at 22% SoA are different findings about different systems, reported separately.
  • We commit to: Reporting confidence intervals on every KPI. If a number has high variance, you will see that variance, not a false point estimate.

5. The Brand Relevance Index (BRI)

The BRI is a composite score (0–100) that summarizes your brand's overall position in the AI layer across four dimensions. It is designed for executive communication, not for methodological analysis — the dimension scores are the analytical substance.

BRI = (AI Visibility × 0.30) + (Authority × 0.30) + (Sentiment × 0.20) + (Narrative × 0.20)

AI Visibility = (SoA × 0.40) + (Intent Visibility × 0.25) + (Shortlist Rate × 0.25) + (Preference Rate × 0.10)
Authority = (Trust Framing × 0.35) + (Citation Rate × 0.30) + (Cross-Source Reinforcement × 0.20) + (Authority Transfer × 0.15)
Sentiment = (Sentiment Balance × 0.45) + (Defensive Vulnerability_inv × 0.35) + (Stability × 0.20)
Narrative = (Topic Association × 0.30) + (Topic Leadership × 0.30) + (Role Share × 0.25) + (Narrative Drift_inv × 0.15)

The weightings reflect a deliberate position: Visibility and Authority are equally important (0.30 each), and together they outweigh Sentiment and Narrative (0.20 each). This reflects the view that presence with credible sourcing is the foundation — everything else is refinement.
