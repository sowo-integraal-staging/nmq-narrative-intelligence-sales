Ch. 1 · Demand Landscape
Chapter 1 · Demand Landscape
“What does AI believe buyers in this category actually want?”
What it illuminates
Before you can measure how your brand is positioned, you need to understand the category itself — through AI's lens. What needs does AI associate with this purchase decision? What does it believe the decision criteria are? What trade-offs does it present to buyers? This is the market context chapter — it tells you how AI frames the problem your brand is solving.
What it enables
You get a ranked map of decision drivers per segment. "For the 35–50 self-employed segment in CH, AI ranks: claims simplicity > digital interface > price > brand reputation." You can compare that against your product's actual strengths. If your product is best-in-class on claims simplicity but AI doesn't surface that as your differentiator in Brand Monitor Ch. 3 — you have a positioning problem you can now pinpoint.
What improves
Content strategy, value proposition clarity, and product-market fit communication.
Decision Driver RankingDecision Driver RankingBefore measuring competitive position, you need to know what the competition is on. DDR reveals which buyer priorities AI believes drive decisions in your category — the criteria against which all brands are evaluated.
Category FrameCategory FrameHow AI defines the boundaries of a category shapes everything — which brands belong, which need types are relevant, which use cases count. If AI frames the category more narrowly or broadly than your product addresses, your positioning will be misaligned with the AI layer.
Segment Need ProfileSegment Need ProfileDifferent buyer segments have fundamentally different need structures. A 28-year-old renter and a 55-year-old CFO are in different markets even when shopping in the same category. SNP makes these differences explicit at the granularity of each defined cohort.
Market Narrative ConsistencyMarket Narrative ConsistencyIf different models give radically different answers about what buyers want in a category, the "AI view of the market" is not stable enough to be a reliable research instrument. MNC measures how much models agree on the demand landscape — the prerequisite for treating results as findings rather than model artefacts.

Ch. 2 · Competitive Perception
Chapter 2 · Competitive Perception
“How does AI rank and frame competitors in buyers' eyes?”
What it illuminates
The demand landscape (Ch. 1) tells you what buyers want. This chapter tells you how AI distributes that demand across the competitive field — who AI positions as the answer to each buyer need, and how. This is the synthetic competitive landscape: not what your sales team thinks of the competition, but how the AI layer has distilled the competitive reality of your market.
What it enables
You can run the same decision-driver prompts from Ch. 1 but ask them comparatively: "Which providers in this category best address X?" You get competitive ranking by driver, by segment, by model. If your primary competitor owns "simplicity" and "digital-first" but nobody owns "specialist coverage for complex risk profiles" — that's a positioning gap you can move into.
What improves
Go-to-market sharpness. Competitive positioning. Clients can track whether positioning investments shift how AI distributes competitive rankings.
Competitive Ranking by Decision DriverCompetitive Ranking by Decision DriverThe decision drivers from Ch. 1 are the competitive battleground. CRDD maps which brands AI believes best address each driver — giving a driver-level competitive landscape rather than a single overall ranking.
Attribute Ownership MapAttribute Ownership MapStrategic positioning is about owning an attribute in the buyer's mind. AOM maps which brand owns which attribute in AI's view of your competitive set — and which attributes are genuinely contested or unclaimed.
White Space IndexWhite Space IndexThe most strategically valuable finding in competitive analysis is not what competitors own but what nobody owns. WSI identifies the decision drivers that buyers care about (from DDR) but that no competitor is clearly associated with in AI's view.
Positioning SharpnessPositioning SharpnessA competitor that is clearly associated with specific attributes is harder to displace than one that is blurry or generic. Positioning Sharpness measures how distinctly each competitor is differentiated in AI's view — high sharpness = owned position; low sharpness = generic, vulnerable.
Segment-level Competitive ShareSegment-level Competitive ShareOverall competitive rankings obscure important segment variation. A brand may dominate overall but underperform in the fastest-growing segment. SCS breaks competitive standing down by cohort to reveal where each competitor is strongest and weakest.

Ch. 3 · Segment Intelligence
Chapter 3 · Segment Intelligence
“How does buyer thinking differ across personas and cohorts?”
What it illuminates
Chapters 1 and 2 give you the market-level view. This chapter breaks that open by segment. The market is not monolithic — a 28-year-old renter in Berlin has a fundamentally different decision architecture than a 55-year-old CFO in Zurich, and AI knows this. This chapter uses the full cohort matrix to reveal how buyer needs, competitive perceptions, and decision drivers shift across persona dimensions: age, profession, geography, life stage, intent type.
What it enables
Segment-level profiles for every cohort: what they want (Ch. 1, broken out per segment), who they prefer (Ch. 2, broken out per segment), and how those things shift with persona dimensions. You can rank segments by strategic opportunity — where is demand high, competitive ownership low, and your product well-matched?
What improves
Targeting precision. Messaging differentiation. ROI on media spend.
Segment Competitive RankingSegment Competitive RankingThe overall competitive ranking from Ch. 2 may hide dramatic segment-level reversals. A brand that is second overall may be first in the highest-value segment. SCR_seg makes the full segment-competitive matrix visible.
Cross-Segment VarianceCross-Segment VarianceIf all segments think the same thing, a single strategy serves everyone. If segments diverge substantially, differentiated strategy is mandatory. CSV quantifies how much segment findings differ — the business case for or against segmented messaging.
Opportunity Score per SegmentOpportunity Score per SegmentNot all segments are equally worth targeting. OSS synthesises three inputs — buyer demand intensity, competitive white space, and product fit — into a single prioritisation score per segment.

Ch. 4 · Signal Consistency
Chapter 4 · Signal Consistency
“When to trust AI as a research instrument — and when to be careful?”
What it illuminates
Product B uses AI as a research instrument — a proxy for human respondents. That claim demands an honest validity layer. This chapter quantifies where AI is a reliable instrument and where it produces noisy, model-specific outputs that should be treated as directional. It separates UnderCurrent from a black box.
What it enables
Cross-Model Consensus (do GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, Mistral agree on the segment findings?), Run-Level Stability (are findings consistent across repeated runs?), Category Validity Score (is this a high-consideration category where AI has rich training data?), and Confidence Bands per KPI. Every deliverable becomes a graded finding.
What improves
Client trust, platform credibility, and reputational integrity.
Cross-Model Consensus ScoreCross-Model Consensus ScoreIf GPT-4, Claude, and Gemini give the same answer about what buyers want, that's a finding. If they give completely different answers, that's a model artefact, not market intelligence. CMCS is the gate between the two.
Run-Level StabilityRun-Level StabilityA finding that changes between the morning run and the afternoon run of the same study is not a finding — it's noise. RLS measures whether the study produces consistent outputs across repeated executions, which is the minimum bar for treating results as research data.
Category Validity ScoreCategory Validity ScoreAI is a more reliable research instrument for high-consideration categories (insurance, banking, B2B software) than for impulse or commodity categories (snacks, fast fashion). CVS rates how well-suited AI is as a research instrument for the specific category being studied.
Confidence Band per Output KPIConfidence Band per Output KPIEvery research finding should carry its own confidence grade. The Confidence Band system converts the underlying quality metrics (CMCS, RLS, CVS) into a per-KPI confidence label that is readable by non-technical stakeholders.
Instrument Bias IndicatorsInstrument Bias IndicatorsDifferent LLMs have known systematic tendencies — some overrepresent US brands, some skew toward large incumbents, some have known political or cultural biases in framing. IBI flags where model-specific biases may be affecting findings.

Ch. 5 · Strategic Implications
Chapter 5 · Strategic Implications
“What would you act on — and why does the data justify it?”
What it illuminates
Market research that doesn't land in strategy is a cost centre. This chapter takes the demand landscape, competitive map, segment intelligence, and confidence gradings from Chapters 1–4 and surfaces the strategic choices they point toward. Not "here is the data" but "here is what the data argues for, stated as decisions."
What it enables
Three outputs: (1) a *Segment Opportunity Ranking* — highest combination of expressed need, low competitive ownership, and product fit; (2) a *Positioning Recommendation* — specific and defensible, grounded in Chapters 1–3; (3) a *Research-to-Action Traceability Map* — every recommendation linked back to the specific data point and confidence grade that supports it. This last output is critical for internal buy-in.
What improves
Decision speed, positioning sharpness, and targeting precision.
Segment Opportunity RankingSegment Opportunity RankingA ranked list of segments by strategic attractiveness — where to play first. SOR synthesises demand intensity, competitive opening, and product fit into a single priority list.
Recommended Positioning StatementRecommended Positioning StatementThe study generates a data-grounded positioning recommendation — a specific, defensible strategic position derived from the intersection of buyer demand (what they want), competitive white space (what's unclaimed), and product fit (what the client can credibly deliver).
Competitive MovesCompetitive MovesStrategy is not just where to play — it's also who to contest and who to concede. Competitive Moves translates the AOM and CRDD data into explicit attack/defend/concede decisions per competitor per segment.
Research-to-Action Traceability MapResearch-to-Action Traceability MapEvery recommendation needs to survive internal scrutiny. The Traceability Map links every strategic recommendation back to the specific KPI finding and confidence grade that supports it — so the client can show their leadership team not just a strategy but its evidence chain.
Confidence-Graded Recommendation SummaryConfidence-Graded Recommendation SummaryA single-page summary that distinguishes hard findings from directional signals — the executive-ready version of the full RTM. Designed for presentation to leadership who need the bottom line, not the full methodology.
